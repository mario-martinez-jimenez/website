Welcome

Visiting Postdoctoral Scholar at Stanford and Imperial College Research Fellow. On the 2025–2026 job market.

I study how early-life shocks, labour-market institutions, and social policy shape health and human capital across the life course. My work blends administrative data with causal-inference methods to inform policies that promote equity and well-being.

Research interests

I collaborate with economists, clinicians, and policymakers to understand how inequality emerges and how policy can close the gap.

  • Long-term effects of early-life shocks
    Evidence on how prenatal health, neonatal care, and early interventions influence children’s adaptive behaviour and human capital.
  • Environmental risks & youth mental health
    Quantifying how pollution, climate events, and economic shocks affect young people’s well-being and resilience.
  • Healthcare access and inequality
    Evaluation of policies that aim to reduce disparities in prevention, treatment, and mental health support.
  • Family formation and fertility dynamics
    Studying how labour-market institutions, temporary contracts, and social policy shape fertility timing and family outcomes.

About

I completed a PhD in Health Policy (Economics) at Lancaster University, supported by the NIHR School for Public Health Research. My academic path includes degrees from Pompeu Fabra University, Erasmus University Rotterdam, and the Autonomous University of Barcelona.

My work appears in journals such as Social Science & Medicine, Economics & Human Biology, BMJ, and Health Economics, Policy and Law. It has also been featured by outlets including The Guardian, The Times, and Nada es Gratis.

At Imperial College London and Lancaster University I have taught across undergraduate and postgraduate programmes, including leadership of the Economics module for the MSc Management programme in 2024–2025.

Affiliations & networks

  • Active member of the Royal Economic Society, the European Labour Economics Society, and the International, European, and Spanish Health Economics Associations.
